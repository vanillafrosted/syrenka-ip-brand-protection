# The Unfamiliar Stamps: What I Learned at the Post Office

## Editor's Note

This article records Ray's experience with Japan's paper-based court filing system as it existed at the time she filed her complaints. Since then, many court filing procedures in Japan have been made electronic. This article is preserved as a historical record of how brand protection litigation proceeded before that transition.

---

## A Moment I Didn't Expect

I had been to the post office hundreds of times before. I knew it well—the usual routine of buying 110-yen stamps for letters, 85-yen stamps for postcards. I'd never needed anything else. Those two denominations had always been enough for my everyday life.

But the court had given me a printed list showing exactly which stamps I needed to buy, and the list included denominations I'd never even seen before: 500-yen stamps, 100-yen stamps, 50-yen stamps, 20-yen stamps, and 10-yen stamps.

The total came to 6,000 yen worth of postage stamps.

When I first read the number, I was genuinely surprised. Not shocked—just surprised. Six thousand yen for stamps? I had expected court costs to exist, but this specific detail—that I would need to physically purchase particular postage stamps in exact quantities—was something I hadn't anticipated.

I made a copy of the court's official list, reduced it to a smaller size, and carefully placed it inside a small plastic sleeve to protect it. Then I put it inside my pouch. Every time I went to the post office to buy the stamps, I would take out that list and show it to the postal clerk.

---

## The Postal Clerk Counting One by One

I remember standing at the post office counter, watching the postal clerk count every single stamp. One by one. Each denomination separated into its own pile.

500-yen stamps: 8 pieces.
110-yen stamps: 10 pieces.
100-yen stamps: 5 pieces.
50-yen stamps: 5 pieces.
20-yen stamps: 5 pieces.
10-yen stamps: 5 pieces.

The clerk moved methodically through each stack, making sure the count was right. I stood there watching, quiet, aware that I was asking for something unusual. Most people weren't coming into the post office to request this specific combination of stamps.

The clerk was patient with me. Professional. No judgment. Just counting each stamp carefully to make sure I had exactly what the court required.

---

## Why These Denominations Mattered

At first, I didn't understand why the court cared about specific stamp denominations. Why not just let people bring money?

But the practical reason became clear once I thought about it: the court needs these stamps to actually mail documents throughout the case. The specific denominations—500 yen, 110 yen, 100 yen, 50 yen, 20 yen, 10 yen—exist because those are the actual Japanese postage rates for different mail types and weights. The court staff would use these stamps to mail copies of my complaint to the other party, and potentially other documents as the case proceeded.

A single 110-yen stamp covers a standard letter within Japan. The smaller denominations allow the court to create exact combinations for different mail weights and services—a package that needs 150 yen in postage could be handled with one 110-yen stamp and one 50-yen stamp, or one 100-yen stamp and one 50-yen stamp.

It was logical, once I understood how the system actually worked.

But it was also completely unlike anything I'd encountered in my everyday life.

---

## Stamps I'd Never Seen Before

This experience taught me how limited my exposure to postage denominations actually was. In everyday life, I'd only ever needed 110-yen and 85-yen stamps. That was it. Those two denominations had covered every postal need I'd ever had.

The 500-yen stamps—those were the most striking. I'd been buying stamps at the post office for years, and I'd never once seen someone purchase a 500-yen stamp. I certainly had never bought one myself. It felt strange holding it, looking at the high denomination, realizing this was how the court system had to function.

The 100-yen, 50-yen, 20-yen, and 10-yen stamps were similarly unfamiliar. I'd passed them every time I went to the post office, but I'd never had a reason to actually buy them. They existed on the charts behind the counter, available for people who needed specific postage combinations.

Now I needed them. All of them. Exactly as the court had specified.

---

## One Concern That Stayed With Me

There was something that worried me as I prepared these stamps: postage stamps cannot be exchanged back for cash once they're purchased. This is just how it works. You can exchange them for other postal products—postcards, for example—but if you do, the post office charges a handling fee. In practical terms, once you buy a stamp, that money is committed.

Because of this, I was especially careful not to buy unnecessary stamps. What if I miscounted? What if I ended up with extra 500-yen stamps that I didn't actually need? I couldn't get that money back. It would be lost.

So I was careful. I checked the court's list multiple times. I showed it to the postal clerk. I watched as they counted each denomination. I made sure I had exactly what I needed, nothing more.

---

## The Two Separate Costs

What I eventually learned was that there were actually two different costs involved in filing a complaint, and they operated through different systems:

**The filing fee** (court fee/裁判所手数料) was paid using revenue stamps—official stamps that represent payment to the court. These came in different denominations based on your claim amount:
- Claims up to 100,000 yen: 1,000 yen in revenue stamps
- Claims up to 500,000 yen: 4,000 yen in revenue stamps
- Claims up to 1,000,000 yen: 8,000 yen in revenue stamps

These revenue stamps had to be glued directly to my complaint document. There was no digital receipt, no payment confirmation other than the physical stamp itself.

**The postage cost** (prepaid postage/予納郵便) was what the court needed to actually mail documents. Instead of me paying a mailing fee when the court sent something, I provided the court with postage stamps in advance. That's what the 6,000 yen worth of stamps were for.

Both had to be purchased separately and attached to my complaint before I could file it.

---

## A Small Tokyo Note

This moment at the post office—standing there with a carefully protected list, watching a clerk count unfamiliar stamps, holding denominations I'd never used before—became one of the defining moments of my brand protection experience.

It represented something important that I hadn't fully understood until that point: the Japanese legal system operates on a different infrastructure than what I was familiar with. It's built on systems that predate digital payments. The courts haven't fully transitioned to modern electronic payment methods. When you file a lawsuit, you're engaging with a system that still relies on physical stamps, paper documents, and in-person visits.

It's not inefficient so much as it is fundamentally different. It requires planning. It requires going to the post office in advance. It requires understanding that certain denominations exist for specific reasons. It requires sitting with the uncertainty that you might not have exactly the right combination until you actually stand there with the postal clerk and verify everything is correct.

But it also means complete transparency. You can see exactly what you're paying. The stamps are tangible evidence. There's no hidden digital transaction or algorithm deciding your fee. It's straightforward: the court tells you what stamps you need, you buy them, you bring them in.

That was how the system worked. That's how I learned brand protection in Japan—one unfamiliar stamp denomination at a time.

## Key Takeaways

- **Filing a court complaint in Japan required two separate stamp purchases**: revenue stamps for the court fee, and postage stamps so the court could mail documents
- **The court specified exact stamp denominations and quantities**: this wasn't negotiable, and the specifications varied by courthouse
- **These denominations existed for practical reasons**: they correspond to actual Japanese postage rates, allowing the court to create exact combinations for different mail types
- **Postage stamps cannot be exchanged for cash** after purchase, only for other postal products (with a handling fee), so careful planning was essential
- **Most people never encounter high-denomination stamps** in everyday life, but the court system requires them
- **This paper-based system reflected an infrastructure** that predates modern electronic payment methods
- **The system required advance planning and in-person visits** to the post office—you couldn't simply pay online

---

## Practical Tips

1. **Call your specific courthouse first** to get the exact stamp denominations and quantities required—this varies by location

2. **Visit a large post office** where they're more likely to have all denominations in stock—smaller post offices may not carry high-value stamps

3. **Bring the court's official list with you** when you visit the post office—postal workers are more helpful when they can see the official requirement

4. **Count carefully with the postal clerk** and verify the count before leaving the counter—once purchased, these stamps are not refundable

5. **Don't buy extra "just in case"** because you cannot get unused stamps refunded—purchase exactly what the court requires

6. **Allow time for this errand** before your filing date—don't try to purchase stamps the morning you plan to submit your complaint

7. **Keep the court's list in a protective sleeve** if you'll be carrying it with you to the post office—the list itself becomes an important reference document

8. **Understand that different courts may have different requirements**—if you file in multiple courts, the stamp requirements may differ

That was the next challenge. But first, I had the stamps. Unfamiliar denominations sitting in front of me. Each one representing a small part of the system I was learning to navigate, one detail at a time.
