# Scene Bible

**Purpose**: The technical language and craft for building scenes in English Syrenka.

This document defines HOW scenes are written. For editorial philosophy, see Editorial Manifesto. For authenticity principles, see Project Vision.

---

## 1. What is a Scene

A scene is not a chronological chunk of time.

A scene is an emotional arc with meaning.

A scene:
- Begins when ordinary life is still ordinary
- Ends when ordinary life is no longer ordinary
- Contains one complete emotional movement
- Is written in present tense
- Invites readers to walk beside Ray

**Key Takeaway**

Organize by emotional truth, not clock time. The scene ends when something has fundamentally shifted.

---

## 2. Reality Before Story

Never invent.

If Ray's real experience is unknown: stop and ask.

If you are uncertain: stop and ask.

If a scene might be more interesting changed: stop and ask.

(For more on authenticity, see Editorial Manifesto and Project Vision.)

**Key Takeaway**

Stop and ask before you guess. Reality is always stronger.

---

## 3. Emotional Arc vs. Chronological Time

Scenes follow emotional arcs, not clock time.

Example: Morning run → Mercari discovery → walking home → trying to work.

Chronologically: several hours.

But emotionally: one complete arc (routine → shock → retreat → stasis).

When organized by emotional arc, readers feel story movement. When organized by clock time, readers feel bored.

**Key Takeaway**

Ask: "What is the emotional truth of this moment?" The answer tells you where the scene begins and ends.

---

## 4. Point of Discovery

Readers discover events exactly when Ray discovers them.

Never ahead. Never behind. Never with future knowledge.

**NOT:** "I knew later that there were multiple sellers, but at first I only saw one."

**YES:** "I see one listing. I go back. I scroll. Another listing. Different seller. Also my photos."

Readers are discovering with Ray. Not reading Ray's summary.

**Key Takeaway**

Stay inside Ray's present experience. Readers stay there with her. They don't know what's coming.

---

## 5. Camera Position

The camera is always close to Ray.

The camera sees what Ray sees.
The camera knows what Ray knows.
The camera never pulls back or jumps ahead.

Keep the camera close. Keep it steady. Keep it with Ray.

**Key Takeaway**

When camera position shifts, readers feel lost. Stay close to Ray. Stay with her.

---

## 6. Present Tense, Immediate Moment

Write as if it's happening now.

**NOT:** "I remember walking home that morning..."

**YES:** "I walk home. Still catching my breath..."

Present tense creates immediacy. Readers are not listening to Ray tell a story. Readers are inside her present moment.

**Key Takeaway**

Present tense is the difference between memoir and lived experience. English Syrenka chooses lived experience.

---

## 7. Reader Walks Beside Ray

The reader is not Ray. The reader walks beside Ray.

(See Editorial Manifesto for full philosophy on this distinction.)

When readers walk beside Ray (not merged), they observe her. Notice her habits. Care about her as a distinct person.

**Key Takeaway**

Separation creates attachment. Readers care about Ray because they observe her as a distinct person they're following.

---

## 8. Protecting Ordinary Moments

Ordinary moments are sacred.

The walk home. Shoes off. Phone on desk. Coffee. Laptop. Typing. Deleting. Phone again. Browser closed. Phone face-down.

These moments are not filler. This is where character lives. This is where readers trust Ray is real.

Protect these moments. Do not rush. Do not skip.

**Key Takeaway**

Ordinary moments ARE the important moments. They're where readers connect with Ray as a real person.

---

## 9. Small Details Matter

Small, authentic details make readers believe.

**NOT:** "I was tired"

**YES:** "I was still catching my breath from the run"

Details don't need explanation. Let readers reach their own conclusions.

**Key Takeaway**

A detail about how Ray holds her phone tells more than a paragraph explaining her feelings. Show, don't explain.

---

## 10. Silence and Space

Not every moment needs words.

Ray scrolls. Ray looks. Ray stops. Ray walks.

Readers feel the silence. Feel the space where words aren't needed.

Silence is presence, not absence. Silence is the most powerful tool a writer has.

**Key Takeaway**

Use silence. It's more powerful than words.

---

## 11. When to Stop a Scene

Stop when the emotional arc is complete.

Not when you've described everything.
Not when you've explained what Ray learned.
Not when you've tied up loose ends.

Stop when something has fundamentally shifted. When Ray cannot return to where she was. When readers have felt the full arc.

Then stop. Let the silence hold.

**Key Takeaway**

Stop when the emotional arc is complete, not when the clock says time's up.

---

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Explaining emotions instead of showing actions**

Mistake: "I felt confused and violated."

Better: "I put the phone face-down. I picked it up. I closed the browser without looking."

Actions reveal emotion. Explanation interrupts.

**Writing by clock time instead of emotional movement**

Mistake: Morning, afternoon, evening (time-based)

Better: Discovery, confusion, avoidance (emotion-based)

**Rushing through ordinary moments**

Mistake: "I walked home and went to my desk."

Better: Stay with each step. Walk. Door. Desk. Phone. Work.

**Explaining what readers should feel**

Mistake: "This moment was devastating."

Better: Show it. Let readers feel it.

**Summarizing future knowledge into past scenes**

Mistake: "I didn't understand then, but later I would learn..."

Better: Stay in the moment. Ray doesn't know what's next. Readers shouldn't either.

**Key Takeaway**

Watch for these patterns. They break immediacy and authenticity.

---

## 13. The Writer's Rule: Improve Language, Never Expand Facts

The writer's role has a clear boundary:

**The writer MAY:**
- Improve the English language and flow
- Make sentences more natural and readable
- Organize Ray's real experience into a stronger narrative
- Polish punctuation, pacing, and readability
- Rearrange Ray's actual events for clearer storytelling

**The writer MUST NOT:**
- Add new events that Ray did not describe
- Invent conversations, reactions, or observations
- Assume what happened between two confirmed events
- Fill gaps in memory with plausible details
- Expand facts beyond what Ray actually experienced

**Example:**

Maki says: "I told the group about the copied listings and they advised me to report them to Mercari."

**The writer may write:** "I speak up. I tell them what I discovered. The group responds immediately. They recognize what this is. They give me the advice: report it to Mercari."

(Improved language, organized narrative, same facts.)

**The writer MUST NOT write:** "I nervously spoke up, my voice shaking slightly as I explained the situation. One of them nodded knowingly and said, 'You need to report this immediately. I had the same thing happen to me last year.' The others chimed in with their own experiences."

(Added invented emotion, invented dialogue, invented reactions not confirmed by Maki.)

**When a Detail is Missing:**

If the writer needs a detail for the scene but it's not supported by Ray's confirmed experience:
- STOP
- Request factual clarification from Maki through the established workflow
- Do NOT invent a plausible detail

**The Principle:**

Readers trust English Syrenka because every event comes from Ray's real experience.

Natural English is encouraged.

Invented reality is not.

**Key Takeaway**

Improve the writing. Never expand the facts. Our readers must trust that everything in English Syrenka is real.

---

## 14. Never Move Facts in Time

Facts are not only accurate—they are also temporally accurate.

Do not collapse multiple events into a single moment for narrative convenience.

Do not rearrange the sequence of when things happened, even if the rearranged sequence would be more dramatic or efficient.

**Example:**

**Inaccurate (moving facts in time):**
"During the seminar, I heard the article numbers and wrote them down: Article 21 and Article 23."

**Accurate (preserving temporal sequence):**
"During the seminar, I heard the article numbers mentioned but couldn't catch them.
Several days later, I watched the recorded seminar again and replayed that section repeatedly until I could write them down: Article 21 and Article 23."

These are two distinct events with different timing. Collapsing them into one moment changes the authenticity of Ray's experience.

**Why This Matters:**

Ray's real experience unfolded in a specific sequence over real time.
- She heard articles mentioned
- She couldn't catch the details
- Days passed
- She watched the recording
- THEN she wrote down the articles

This temporal arc is part of her story. Moving the "writing down" into the seminar itself removes a meaningful part of her real experience: the effort of reviewing the recording, the patience of replaying it, the work of catching details she initially missed.

**Key Takeaway**

Preserve temporal accuracy. Do not move facts in time for narrative convenience. Ray's real experience unfolds in real sequence, and that sequence matters.

## Editorial Goal

The purpose of every scene is not to impress readers.

It is to make readers feel they are quietly spending time beside Ray.

Beside a real person living her real life.

**The only test that matters:**

Does this scene make readers feel they're spending time with a real person?

If yes: the scene is complete.

If no: rewrite it.

**Key Takeaway**

You're building a window, not writing literature. Readers should forget they're looking through glass and simply feel they're standing beside Ray.
