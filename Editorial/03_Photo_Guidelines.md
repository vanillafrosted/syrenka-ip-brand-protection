# Photo Guidelines

**Purpose**: The technical language and craft for how photographs serve English Syrenka.

This document defines HOW photographs work. For editorial philosophy on authenticity, see Project Vision and Editorial Manifesto.

---

## 1. Why Photos Exist

Photos exist to anchor readers in Ray's lived experience.

A photograph says: "This moment happened. This place is real. I was here."

Photos are proof. Not decoration. Not illustration.

When readers see a photograph, they should feel: "I am deeper inside Ray's day. This place is real. Ray stood here."

**Key Takeaway**

Photos prove Ray's day is real. They anchor readers in lived experience, not imagination.

---

## 2. Photos as Scene Transitions

Every photograph is a scene transition.

A photo marks the moment when readers move with Ray from one part of her day to another.

Without the photo, readers might skip over the transition. With the photo, readers experience it alongside Ray.

Photos create natural pauses where readers can slow, absorb, and move forward together with Ray.

Photos are not interruptions. Photos are part of the story's rhythm.

**Key Takeaway**

Photos mark transitions. They let readers breathe and move with Ray into the next moment.

---

## 3. Choosing the Right Moment

The story chooses the photograph.

The photograph never chooses the story.

A photograph is chosen because:
- Ray was there during this moment
- This place matters to her journey
- Readers need to see this to understand what comes next
- The emotional timing is right

A photograph is NOT chosen because:
- It is famous or iconic
- It is visually beautiful
- It would look good in a gallery

**The only question:** Does this photo belong here in Ray's story?

**Key Takeaway**

Story chooses photo, not the other way around. Authentic moments matter more than beautiful images.

---

## 4. Authenticity Standard

Every photograph must be authentic.

No stock photos. No AI-generated images. No borrowed images.

Every photograph shows:
- A real place Ray actually goes
- An object Ray actually uses
- A moment Ray actually experiences
- Tokyo as Ray actually sees it

(For more on authenticity, see Editorial Manifesto and Project Vision.)

Authentic photographs might be imperfect. The lighting might be ordinary. The composition simple. That is exactly right. Ray's day is not award-winning. Ray's day is real.

**Key Takeaway**

Imperfect and real is always stronger than perfect and invented. Authenticity matters more than technical quality.

---

## 5. Photo Placeholders

While articles are being written, photos are represented as placeholders.

Format: [Photo Placeholder: Description of what would be photographed]

Examples:
- [Photo Placeholder: Ray's waist pouch and phone after morning run]
- [Photo Placeholder: Convenience store entrance where Ray prints documents]
- [Photo Placeholder: Ray's desk at home with phone face-down]

Placeholders serve two purposes:
1. Show exactly where a photograph should appear
2. Describe what the photograph should show

Placeholders are specific and detailed. They tell the photographer exactly what needs to be captured.

**Key Takeaway**

Placeholders are precise instructions for photographers. They describe moments, not concepts.

---

## 6. Real Photographs

When placeholders become real photographs:

**Positioning:** Appears exactly where the placeholder was positioned. Never moved or repositioned.

**Framing:** Shows what Ray actually sees. Not an artistic angle. Not staged. What Ray actually sees.

**Context:** Includes enough context so readers understand where Ray is and why she's showing them this place.

**Simplicity:** Does not need to be perfect. Simple, clear, authentic is better than technically perfect.

**Caption:** A brief caption (1-2 sentences) explaining why this moment matters.

Example caption: "The convenience store printer where I began collecting documents for my first court filing. This ordinary machine became crucial to my brand protection journey."

**Key Takeaway**

Real photographs should be simple, positioned exactly where the placeholder was, and captioned from Ray's perspective about why the moment matters.

---

## 7. Privacy & Ethics

Photographs must protect Ray's identity and privacy.

**What photos should show:**
- Places Ray works (convenience stores, post offices, courts)
- Objects Ray uses
- Tokyo as Ray experiences it
- Moments in Ray's professional journey

**What photos must NOT show:**
- Ray's face (in most cases)
- Identifying information about Ray's business
- Names or faces of defendants
- Specific personal details that reveal Ray's identity
- Anything that compromises Ray's safety

The goal: Readers experience Ray's places and moments without Ray being exposed.

**Key Takeaway**

Bring readers into Ray's day without exposing Ray herself. Protect her privacy completely.

---

## 8. Caption Style

Captions are written from Ray's perspective.

Captions are brief (1-2 sentences maximum).

Captions explain why this photograph matters to Ray's journey—not what the photograph shows.

**Weak caption** (describes what's visible): "A convenience store printer in Tokyo."

**Strong caption** (explains why it matters): "Where I learned that ordinary tools could help me protect my brand. This printer became my first defense system."

Captions use active voice. Captions are personal. Captions connect the photo to Ray's emotional and practical journey.

**Key Takeaway**

Captions should explain why the photo matters, not describe what it shows. Write from Ray's perspective.

---

## 9. What Not to Photograph

Do not photograph places simply because they are famous, iconic, or visually striking.

**Don't photograph:**
- Tokyo Tower (just because it's iconic)
- Famous shrines (unless Ray actually prayed there)
- Recognizable landmarks (unless they're part of Ray's story)
- Stereotypical "Tokyo" scenes

**Instead photograph:**
- The convenience store where Ray printed documents
- The supermarket where Ray celebrated
- The post office where Ray bought revenue stamps
- The train platform where Ray waited nervously

The ordinary places where Ray's life actually happened are more valuable than all famous landmarks.

Authenticity beats tourism.

**Key Takeaway**

Photograph Ray's ordinary life, not Tokyo's famous landmarks. Story authenticity always wins.

---

## 10. Emotional Timing

Photographs create rhythm in the article.

A photo allows readers to pause, breathe, absorb, and transition.

Photographs should appear when:
- Readers need to step into a new physical space
- Ray's emotional state shifts
- A significant moment deserves visual weight
- Readers need to process before moving forward

Photographs should NOT appear:
- Too frequently
- Too rarely
- At random moments
- To fill space or meet a quota

A photograph is a natural pause. It should feel right. Not too early. Not too late. Exactly when the story needs it.

**Key Takeaway**

Photos create rhythm. Readers should feel the pause is exactly right—part of the story's natural flow.

---

## 11. The Editorial Test

Before using any photograph, ask this single question:

**"If this photograph disappeared, would the story lose something?"**

Answer "No" → do not use the photo.

Answer "Yes" → the photo belongs.

Use this test every time. Use it for every photograph. Use it with honesty.

The story is stronger than any single image.

But when a photograph truly belongs, the story becomes even stronger.

**Key Takeaway**

One question decides it: Would the story lose something if this photo disappeared? That's the only test.

---

## 12. Final Principle

The goal of photography is not to create a beautiful photo gallery.

The goal is to help readers experience Ray's day as naturally and completely as possible.

Readers should feel that each photograph is exactly where it needs to be.

Nothing should feel decorative. Everything should feel lived.

When a reader remembers a photograph later, they should remember it because it belonged to the story—not because it was beautiful.

**Key Takeaway**

Photos should feel lived, not decorative. Readers should remember them because they mattered to Ray's story, not because they were pretty.
