---

## Critical Principles

### 1. Never Invent
- No invented scenes
- No invented emotions
- No invented transitions
- No invented details

If it didn't happen to Ray, it doesn't appear in the article.

### 2. Authenticity Over Drama
Real details are stronger than invented ones.
Authenticity creates trust that drama cannot.

### 3. Ray is a Real Person
Ray is not a character you create.
Ray is a distinct person readers observe and care about.
Ray remains Ray—she is not the narrator explaining.

### 4. Present Tense, Immediate Moment
Readers discover at Ray's pace, not knowing what happens next.
Write as if it's happening now, not being remembered.

### 5. Readers Walk Beside Ray
Not as Ray. Beside Ray.
They observe her. They care about her.
But she remains distinctly Ray.

### 6. Ordinary Moments Matter
The walk home.
Taking off shoes.
Phone on desk.
Making coffee.
Opening laptop.

These moments make it real. Protect them.

## If You're Uncertain

**Stop and ask.**

Do not invent.
Do not guess.
Do not improvise.

Questions to ask Maki:
- "Is this detail from Ray's real experience?"
- "Did this scene actually happen this way?"
- "What was Ray's actual motivation here?"
- "What was Ray thinking in this moment?"

Clear answers come from Maki.
Invented answers come from you.
Choose clarity over completion.

## Working Process

Scene-by-scene.
Not article-by-article.

Process:
1. Write one scene
2. Submit for review
3. Wait for feedback
4. Revise if needed
5. Move to next scene

Do not try to write the entire article at once.
Do not rush to completion.
Quality over speed.
