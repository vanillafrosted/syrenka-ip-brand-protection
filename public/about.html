Our Mission

Syrenka exists to share real experiences of protecting a small brand in Japan. This is not a legal advice website. It's a blog where someone who has been where you are—overwhelmed, uncertain, and trying to protect something she created—shares what she learned along the way.

My Story

I found the listing by accident, on my phone, still catching my breath from my morning jog. I was scrolling through search results for my own product when I saw it: my image, my design, my packaging—listed under someone else's name on a platform I'd never sold to.

I didn't feel angry at first. I felt disbelief. I remember standing in my hallway, still in my running shoes, reading the listing over and over, as if reading it again would somehow explain it differently. Then I put the phone down. I couldn't keep looking at it.

I'd made six product images for that listing, and each one had taken real time—hours of arranging, photographing, redoing shots. A couple of those images even had me in them. Seeing those same images on someone else's listing made it feel like all of that effort had simply been taken away.

For several days, I didn't tell anyone. I just went about my work, pretending everything was fine while that listing sat in my mind like a weight I couldn't put down. The hurt softened into something heavier—a quiet misery that I'd catch myself thinking about while washing dishes, packing orders, lying awake at night.

Eventually a friend noticed something was off. When she asked if I'd reported it, I realized I hadn't. Part of me didn't know where to start, but a bigger part was afraid: what if I tried and nothing happened? What if being ignored made me feel even more miserable than doing nothing at all?

Then I attended an online seminar about copyright. The speaker warned the audience about the serious consequences of copyright infringement—that a copyright holder could send a certified letter demanding compensation, and if ignored, the matter could proceed to a lawsuit. I listened the same way everyone else did.

But before the seminar even finished, something shifted. Sitting there listening, I suddenly realized: I'm not the person the speaker is warning. I'm on the opposite side. I'm the person whose work was copied. I could be the one sending that certified letter.

My heart raced as it dawned on me: I'm not the infringer. I have options. I have a tool I could use.

I hesitated for several more days. Then I wrote the letter. I brought it to the post office, paid the fee, had it notarized, and sent it to the seller's registered address. I don't remember feeling triumphant. Mostly I remember feeling like I'd finally exhaled after holding my breath for a long time.

That letter was the beginning of everything that followed.

You Are Not Alone

If you're reading this because something you created was copied and you don't know what to do, I understand that feeling. I've been there. I know what it's like to feel helpless and unsure.

The difference between where I was and where you are now is simple: I have a few more stories. I've made a few more mistakes and learned from them. But I didn't start as someone who knew what to do. I started as someone who was hurt, overwhelmed, and desperate enough to try something—anything—to protect what mattered to me.

That's what I want you to know: you don't need to feel ready. You don't need to understand everything. You don't need to be fearless or brilliant or perfectly prepared.

You just need to care enough to take the next small step.

Everything else follows from there.
