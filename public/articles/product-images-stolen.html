When Your Product Images Get Stolen: What Actually Happened

I was checking Mercari one afternoon when something stopped me.

I saw my product image. Not just any image—one of the images I'd created specifically for Amazon. Amazon-exclusive photos, with Amazon-specific elements, on a platform that had nothing to do with Amazon.

That didn't make sense.

I stared at the screen, trying to understand how that was even possible. I'd never listed this product on Mercari. I'd never uploaded these images anywhere except Amazon. So how were they here?

I scrolled down and saw it again. And again. The same images on multiple different Mercari Shops listings.

Multiple sellers. All using my Amazon-exclusive images.

I felt confused more than anything else. Not angry yet. Just confused. Like I was missing some obvious explanation.

Then I looked more carefully at one of the listings and saw my own face looking back at me.

It was one of my product images where I was holding the awards I'd earned after using this product. I'd included that photograph in my listing specifically because it was proof. Not just a product photo. Evidence. Real evidence that I actually used the product and achieved real results.

Now a stranger was using my face, my achievements, my awards—to sell their version of a product they'd never used themselves.

That's when confusion turned into something else.

When It's Not Just Your Images—It's Your Credibility

Here's what shocked me: the hurt wasn't about losing sales. It was about what they were selling by using my achievements.

That photograph with the awards wasn't just a product photo. It was evidence. Real evidence that I had actually used this product and achieved real results through my own effort and experience. I'd earned those achievements. They were mine.

And now multiple sellers on Mercari were using my face, my awards, my credibility to sell their products. Products they'd never tested. Results they'd never earned.

They weren't just copying my images. They were borrowing my achievements. My experience. My earned trust. My proof that the product works.

The anger I felt was different than I expected. It wasn't about the money. It was about the lie they were telling by using my face and my accomplishments. Customers thought these sellers had achieved what I had achieved. They thought these sellers had my experience. They thought these sellers had earned the trust that I had earned.

They had none of that. They just had my photograph.

That felt like theft on a different level entirely. They weren't stealing just an image. They were stealing my credibility. My reputation. The trust I had built through my own real experience. And in doing so, they were deceiving their customers—making them believe that someone had earned achievements they had never earned.

I didn't have words for what I felt. I didn't know the legal concepts yet. I just knew something was deeply, fundamentally wrong.

Trying to Figure Out What to Do

I didn't know anything about copyright or trademark law at that point. I didn't know what I was allowed to do. I didn't even know if what was happening was illegal.

I reported it to Amazon. They said they'd look into it. Then nothing happened. The listing stayed up.

I waited a few weeks, checked back, and it was still there.

So I reported it again.

This time, nothing happened faster.

I remember feeling really helpless. I'd created this work. I could see someone using it. And I had no idea how to make it stop.

That's when I started wondering: what if I made my images differently?

The First Thing I Tried

I decided to add my logo to my product images.

It seemed simple enough. If my logo was on there, anyone looking at the photo would know it belonged to me. And if someone stole it, it would be obvious they'd stolen it—my name would be right there.

I'm not sure if this would actually help, honestly. But I had to try something.

So I created a version of my logo and started putting it on every new product photo I took. Not huge and obnoxious. Just visible enough that you could see it was part of the image.

I also decided to create images specifically for Amazon. I wouldn't use the same photos on other platforms. That way, if I saw my image somewhere else, I'd know for sure it was stolen—it would have Amazon-specific elements in it that made no sense anywhere else.

I started doing these things. I'm not sure how much it actually helped—I couldn't go back and check if fewer images were stolen because I was too busy running my business. But it felt like I was doing something.

My First Real Problem

Then something happened that made me realize how much deeper this issue went.

I discovered that someone had copied one of my listings including the images where my face was visible.

My actual face. In their listing. Being used to sell their version of my product.

That wasn't just copyright infringement or whatever. That was using my image to sell something. That was different.

I felt violated in a way I hadn't before.

I don't remember exactly what I did at that point—I think I just panicked and reported it everywhere I could. But nothing happened. The listing stayed up.

At some point, I realized I needed to actually understand what my options were. So I started reading about trademark and copyright. I started talking to people who'd been through similar situations.

And very slowly, I began to understand that there might be a legal path forward if I documented things properly and built a case.

That's when I decided to file my first actual legal complaint.

Learning From Actually Fighting Back

I won't go into all the details of that first case—it was messy, I made mistakes, I didn't know what I was doing. But I learned something crucial from it:

The court cared about proof that something was mine from the start.

My logo in the image? That mattered. The fact that the image had my specific design choices? That mattered. Evidence that I'd created it on a specific date? That mattered.

But generic product photos with no identifying marks? Those were harder to prove were mine.

The judge asked me questions like: "How do you know you created this and not the defendant?"

And suddenly I realized: I needed to have been more intentional about marking my work as mine from the beginning.

Trying Something Different

After that case, I started approaching my images completely differently.

I stopped just arranging products and taking photos. I started being deliberate about making the images recognizably mine.

I began taking my own photographs instead of sourcing generic product images. I made my own design choices about backgrounds, angles, styling. I added custom graphics—things I created, not templates everyone could use. I chose specific fonts and colors and used them consistently across all my images.

I was basically trying to make it so that if someone copied my image, they weren't just copying a photo. They were copying something I'd clearly put creative work into.

I wasn't even sure this would help legally. But I was learning, slowly, that the more obviously creative something was, the easier it was to defend.

It took time. I was still running my business. I was still dealing with copied listings. But I was learning as I went, trying different approaches, seeing what seemed to work better.

The Bigger Realization

At some point—I can't pinpoint exactly when—I realized something.

When I included my own photograph in product images, things changed.

Not just legally, but practically. Sellers who might copy a generic product photo thought twice about copying an image with my face on it. Because copying my face meant they were literally stealing my likeness. That was different. That was meaner, in a way.

So I started including myself in at least some of my images. Not as a model, but as proof. "This product is real. This person made it. This is not some generic item."

And fewer of those images got copied.

Looking Back and Seeing the Pattern

I've been writing this trying to explain what happened, and I'm realizing something as I write.

I didn't have a plan. I wasn't strategically building layers of protection.

I was just... trying things. Discovering what worked. Running into problems and then asking "what if I did this instead?"

But looking back now at everything I tried—adding my logo, making creative design choices, including my own image—I can see something I didn't see while I was doing it:

There were actually three different approaches I ended up using. And they got progressively stronger.

The first thing I did—adding identifying marks like logos and consistent visual design—made my images obviously mine. It protected my trademark in a way that was simple and clear.

Then I moved to making the images more creatively distinctive—my own photography, my own design choices, my own artistic input. This protected the creative work I'd actually done.

Finally, I started including my own image. My face. My person. That made it so copying my images wasn't just about stealing product photos. It was about stealing me.

Each level was something I discovered because the previous level wasn't quite enough. Not because I had some master plan.

What I Know Now (But Didn't Know Then)

I'm telling you this because I wish someone had explained this to me when I was first starting.

Not as three neat strategies. But as three natural steps I could take—each one simple enough to do, but each one stronger than the last.

You don't need to do all three. You don't need to have it all figured out from the start.

But if you're worried about your images being copied—and if you're selling a product you care about, you probably should be—you could start with the simplest thing: marking your work as yours with a logo or consistent visual style.

And if that's not enough, you know where to go next.

I learned this all through trial and error. Through cases that I wasn't sure I'd win. Through spending nights reading about copyright law when I barely understood what I was reading.

But I learned it. And now you don't have to figure it out alone.

Key Takeaways

  • When your product images get stolen, the hurt isn't just about sales—it's about time and creativity being taken
  • Adding identifying marks to your images (logos, consistent design) makes them recognizably yours from the start
  • Creating images with your own creative input (your photography, your design choices) makes them stronger to defend
  • Including your own image in product photos adds a personal layer that most counterfeiters avoid
  • Each level of protection emerged from discovering that the previous approach wasn't quite enough
  • You don't need to have a perfect system before you start—you can build it as you learn
  • The court system cares about proof that something was yours from the beginning
  • The more intentional you are about marking your work as yours, the less likely it is to be stolen in the first place

Practical Tips

Tip 1: Start Somewhere Simple

Add your logo to your next product image. That's it. You don't need a perfect plan. You don't need to transform everything today. Add one identifying mark and see how it feels. See how it changes how you think about your work.

Tip 2: Pick a Visual Style and Stick With It

Choose 1-2 colors. Choose a font. Choose a layout approach. Use the same style across all your images. This isn't about being fancy—it's about being recognizably you. When customers see your images, they should instantly know they're from you.

Tip 3: Take At Least One Photo Yourself

You don't have to photograph everything. But create at least one product image where the photo is genuinely yours—an angle only you thought of, a moment only you captured. This makes your listing much harder to copy wholesale.

Tip 4: Save Your Original Files

Keep the original image files. Keep records of when you created them. Keep evidence of your creative process if you can. If you ever need to prove something in court, these records matter more than you'd think.

Tip 5: Remember This Is a Process

You don't need to do everything at once. You're learning as you go. Try one thing. See what you learn. Then try the next thing. That's how I figured this out, and it works.
