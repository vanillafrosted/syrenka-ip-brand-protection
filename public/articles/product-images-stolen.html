When Your Product Images Get Stolen: What Actually Happened

I found the listing by accident, on my phone, still catching my breath from my morning jog.

I hadn't even sat down yet. I checked my phone the way I always do after a run—messages first, then a quick look at how my own listings were doing. I was scrolling through search results for my own product, the way I sometimes do just to see how it looks to customers.

And there it was. On Mercari.

My Amazon-exclusive product images. The ones I'd created specifically for Amazon. The ones with Amazon-specific elements. On a completely different platform.

I couldn't understand it. I'd never listed this product on Mercari. I'd never uploaded these images anywhere except Amazon. How were they here?

I scrolled down and saw it again. Multiple listings. Multiple different Mercari Shops sellers. All using my Amazon-exclusive images.

Then I saw my own face. One of my product images where I was wearing the gold medal I'd earned, holding the winner's trophy I'd won after using this product myself. That photograph was proof—real, tangible evidence that I actually used the product and achieved real results.

A stranger was now using my face, my medal, my trophy to sell a product they'd never used.

At first, I just felt confused. Like I was missing something obvious. Like there had to be an explanation.

But there wasn't.

When It's Not Just Your Images—It's Your Credibility

The photograph with my gold medal and trophy wasn't just a product photo. It was proof. Real proof that I had actually used this product and achieved real results.

Now multiple sellers on Mercari were using my face, my medal, my trophy to sell their products. Products they'd never used. Results they'd never earned.

That wasn't just copying. They were using my credibility—my actual achievements—to gain trust they hadn't earned. Customers looking at that photograph would believe these sellers had done what I had done.

I knew this was wrong. I knew it was illegal. What I didn't know was whether there was anything I could realistically do about it.

What Could I Actually Do About It?

I knew this was wrong. I knew copying someone else's images was illegal. I understood that much.

What I didn't know was whether there was anything I could realistically do about it.

Copying happened everywhere online. Many people seemed to accept it as just part of doing business. Reports to platforms often went nowhere. I felt helpless—not because I didn't understand the law, but because I didn't know if taking action would actually change anything.

At that point, I had already been placing my own logos on many of my product images. Some were clear logos. Some were watermark-style logos. I'd included them because I believed that adding visible branding might discourage people from copying.

When I discovered that sellers had copied these images anyway—even with my logos still clearly visible—I was genuinely shocked.

If visible branding and clear logos didn't stop people from copying, what would?

That question became my starting point for thinking more deeply about protecting my product images.

What I Know Now (But Didn't Know Then)

I wish I could tell you I had a master plan. But the truth is, I learned this the hard way—through many copied listings, through many legal cases, through listening to defendants explain why they thought they could get away with using my images.

Some of the things I heard surprised me. Defendants would claim they didn't know copying was illegal. They would claim they thought anyone's product photos were fair to use. Some seemed genuinely confused about the difference between using an image and creating an image.

Every case taught me something. Every excuse made me understand a little more about what I needed to do differently.

And very slowly, I realized something important:

Reducing the chance of being copied is important. But preparing yourself so you can confidently assert your rights after copying occurs is just as important.

Because copying will happen. You can mark your images, you can add logos, you can do everything right—and someone will still copy you. That's not a failure. That's just part of running a business that's worth copying.

The real question isn't "How do I prevent being copied?" The real question is "If I'm copied, will I be ready?"

And here's what helped me most: You don't have to be a lawyer. The Japanese court system actually assumes that ordinary people don't know the law. That's not a disadvantage—that's built into how the system works. The judge doesn't expect you to quote precedent. The judge expects you to know your own work and be able to explain what's yours.

That understanding gave me the confidence to keep going, even when I wasn't sure what I was doing.

Key Takeaways

  • When your product images get stolen, the hurt isn't just about sales—it's about time and creativity being taken
  • Adding identifying marks to your images (logos, consistent design) makes them recognizably yours from the start
  • Creating images with your own creative input (your photography, your design choices) makes them stronger to defend
  • Including your own image in product photos adds a personal layer that most counterfeiters avoid
  • Each level of protection emerged from discovering that the previous approach wasn't quite enough
  • You don't need to have a perfect system before you start—you can build it as you learn
  • The court system cares about proof that something was yours from the beginning
  • The more intentional you are about marking your work as yours, the less likely it is to be stolen in the first place

Practical Tips

Tip 1: Start Somewhere Simple

Add your logo to your next product image. That's it. You don't need a perfect plan. You don't need to transform everything today. Add one identifying mark and see how it feels. See how it changes how you think about your work.

Tip 2: Pick a Visual Style and Stick With It

Choose 1-2 colors. Choose a font. Choose a layout approach. Use the same style across all your images. This isn't about being fancy—it's about being recognizably you. When customers see your images, they should instantly know they're from you.

Tip 3: Take At Least One Photo Yourself

You don't have to photograph everything. But create at least one product image where the photo is genuinely yours—an angle only you thought of, a moment only you captured. This makes your listing much harder to copy wholesale.

Tip 4: Save Your Original Files

Keep the original image files. Keep records of when you created them. Keep evidence of your creative process if you can. If you ever need to prove something in court, these records matter more than you'd think.

Tip 5: Remember This Is a Process

You don't need to do everything at once. You're learning as you go. Try one thing. See what you learn. Then try the next thing. That's how I figured this out, and it works.
