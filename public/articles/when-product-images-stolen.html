When Your Product Images Get Stolen: What It Actually Takes to Fight Back

A real journey from discovery to first action

← Back to Home ← Back to Articles
By Ray Kono
Tokyo, Japan
Prototype Article No.1 | English Syrenka

I wake between 4:30 and 5:00 a.m. I don't set an alarm.

By 5:30, I'm running.

I walk to the park. I jog. While I run, I think about my products. I think about work I need to do today. I think about new product ideas. I think about what I'll eat for lunch and dinner. I think about a meeting. My mind moves through many things while I run.

When I finish, my breathing is heavy. My heart is beating fast. I'm sweating. It feels hard when I finish.

After a few moments, I feel better. Physically better. My mind is clearer too.

I walk home.

Around the park gate, I pull out my phone. I check my Amazon sales. This is something I do every morning.

Then I look at Mercari.

I see a listing. My product. My images. I created these images a month ago. I remember them clearly.

Why is this on Mercari?

I look at the search results again. Another listing. Different seller. Also my photos.

I find three listings total.

I stand there looking at my phone for about one minute.

I put my phone into my waist pouch and zip it closed. I walk home.

The Ordinary Day That Changes Everything

At home, I pick up my mail. I open it and check it. I wash my hands. I drink cold water. I take a shower.

Then I sit at my desk.

I open my email. I check it.

I pick up my phone several times during the day. I don't look at Mercari. I try not to look at those listings. I could look, but I don't want to.

I look out the window. I see the buildings. The sky.

I make coffee. I drink more coffee than I usually do. I sit back at my desk.

I try to work. I type a few sentences. I stop and look at the window again. I delete what I wrote and start over.

The copied listings stay on my mind all day.

I don't look at Mercari again for the rest of the day.

I work. I make coffee. I check email. I do the ordinary things. The listings stay in my mind, but I don't return to them. I've decided—for now—not to look.

The evening comes. I eat. I sit at my desk a while longer. Then I stop working and close my laptop.

The Meeting That Changes Everything

The next evening, I prepare for the online meeting.

It's a regular meeting. Four or five of us. We are all Amazon sellers. We all sell our own brand products on Amazon Japan. We started meeting regularly a few months ago to share experiences, ask questions, compare what we're learning.

I join the online meeting.

I'm the first to arrive. I started this meeting a few months ago, so I'm usually here first, waiting for the others.

We start talking about sales, about our recent weeks, about what's happening with our products.

Then one of the sellers starts talking about his experience.

His brand products have been copied on Amazon Japan. He discovered this a few weeks ago. He's been dealing with it since then.

He explains what happened. How he found the copies. How many there were. What he tried to do about it.

The group listens. The conversation continues. This seller shares what he's learned. The discussion about copied products goes on for a while.

I sit and listen.

And I think about my own situation. The Mercari listings. The photos that are mine.

When that conversation finally ends, there's a small silence. Then someone starts talking about something else.

But I don't follow that conversation.

I'm thinking about what I just heard. About this seller's experience. About how real this is. About how it's not just something that happens to other people—it's happening to me too.

And I think: I should tell them.

I wasn't planning to say anything. I was still in the avoiding phase. But listening to this seller talk about his copies, listening to the group take it seriously, I realize I should tell them about mine.

I speak up.

I tell them that my product images have been copied on Mercari. I explain what I found. Three listings with my photos.

The group responds. They tell me to report the listings to Mercari.

They recognize I haven't done anything yet. They recognize that without this advice, I probably wouldn't have done anything.

I hear their advice.

And in that moment—sitting in that online meeting, listening to people I trust tell me this is serious and this is what I need to do—something shifts.

I become convinced. Not just worried. Not just confused.

Convinced that the copied listings are wrong. Convinced that this is serious enough. Convinced that I need to report them.

The meeting continues, but for me, something has already changed.

I need to do something about this.

My First Attempt

After the online meeting, I don't report the listings.

Instead, I try another approach first.

I leave a comment for one Mercari seller instead of reporting the listing to Mercari.

The seller replies with a formal, almost routine response and removes the listing.

Through that experience, I realize something important.

If a seller blocks me on Mercari, I can no longer see that seller's listings.

I lose the ability to monitor future infringement.

At that moment, I realize that contacting sellers one by one is not an effective long-term strategy for protecting my brand.

The Waiting

I don't do anything.

The realization about blocking and monitoring stays with me, but it doesn't push me to act. Instead, it makes me feel that doing something one by one is pointless. A waste of time.

So I don't.

But I still check.

I still open Mercari. I still search for my product using the same keywords. I look at the copied listings again.

This time, I look more carefully. I notice things I didn't see before. The listings. The sellers. The details.

But after I look, I still don't do anything.

The days pass.

The Seminar

Several days later, I see an announcement for an online copyright seminar.

It's being broadcast live from a venue. I can join online and watch.

I decide to attend.

I join the seminar online while it's happening, watching from my screen.

The seminar starts on a weekend afternoon, after lunch.

I join on my screen. The speaker is a lawyer. He begins with the basics of copyright.

Then he shifts. He starts explaining what people should be careful not to do.

Don't copy someone else's images. Don't post them on X or other platforms. Don't modify them slightly and think that makes it okay. Even a small change may still infringe someone else's copyright.

He mentions specific articles of Japanese copyright law. I can't catch the names or the article numbers. The legal language is unfamiliar. I'm listening, but I don't really understand it.

Then the speaker gives a warning.

His voice shifts. This is the serious part.

If someone infringes another person's copyright, the copyright holder may send a certified letter. A letter demanding compensation. A formal demand.

If the person who received that letter doesn't resolve it—if they ignore it or refuse to fix it—the matter may proceed to a lawsuit.

This is the warning. This is what he wants people to understand.

At that moment, something changes.

I'm sitting here listening to this warning, and a thought begins to form.

Wait.

This warning isn't for me.

I'm not the person the speaker is warning.

I'm not the person who needs to be afraid of receiving a certified letter from someone else.

I'm the person whose work has been copied.

I'm the person whose copyright has been infringed.

I'm on the opposite side.

The words "certified letter" stay in my mind. I keep thinking about them.

And then another thought arrives.

I'm the person who can send that letter.

My heart begins to beat faster.

I keep listening to the rest of the seminar, but something inside me has already shifted.

After it ends, I sit with this thought.

I'm seriously beginning to think about it now.

Should I actually send a certified letter?

Preparing the Letter

The next day, I pull up the recorded seminar.

I want to know the names of the copyright laws the lawyer mentioned. I couldn't catch them during the live talk, and they stayed with me. The speaker was a lawyer, so I believe those legal references would be correct. If I'm going to send a certified letter, I want to use the same legal references he mentioned.

I find the section where he talks about copyright law.

I replay it. Once. Twice. Just that section.

I pause the recording.

I write down what I hear:

Article 21
Article 23

I write them on a piece of paper by hand. The article numbers. That's what I catch.

I still don't really understand what those laws actually mean. I look at the titles. Right of Reproduction. Right of Public Transmission. I guess their general meaning from the titles themselves.

But I don't study the laws deeply. I don't read the full text. I simply believe that these must be the right references. The lawyer mentioned them. They must matter.

And I think about this: if I include these legal references in my certified letter, it might make the letter more convincing.

At this moment, I still haven't written the certified letter.

I hold the paper with the two article numbers.

There is still so much to do.

I have to learn how to write a certified letter.

I have to learn the formatting rules.

I have to decide how much compensation to ask for.

I have to collect screenshots of the copied listings as evidence.

Everything is new to me.

Everything will take time.

Gathering Evidence

Now begins the real work.

I have to learn how to prepare a certified letter.

I research everything that seems important:

How does a certified letter actually work? Where do I send one? How much will it cost? What are the strict formatting rules—the exact number of characters per line, the number of lines per page? What information should I include in the letter itself? How do I decide an appropriate amount to ask for as compensation?

Everything is new.

I also begin collecting evidence.

I take many screenshots of the copied listings. Each screenshot is saved carefully. I create a new Google document for this case. I organize everything inside it.

And I create a simple sheet to track everything. Just a basic organization system at first. I think of it as nothing more than a way to stay organized.

I also request the seller information from Mercari. The seller's registered address. Their details. I need this for the certified letter. Some time later, Mercari sends it to me by email.

The screenshots take much longer than I expected.

At first, I don't know which parts of each listing will actually matter as evidence. Which image? Which text? Which price information? I spend a lot of time deciding what to capture. What matters? What doesn't?

There are many screenshots.

And while I'm doing this—taking them, organizing them, arranging them—a thought keeps appearing in my mind:

Is this really worth spending so much time on?

I'm not completely convinced that all of this work will actually help. I don't know if any of this will matter. But I keep going anyway.

Writing the Letter

I open Google Docs.

I open the Japan Post website on another window. I keep the official instructions visible while I write. I want to follow every rule.

Before I even finish the first sentence, I make a decision.

This letter needs to sound formal. It needs to feel like a legal document. When the reader opens it, they need to understand this is not an ordinary complaint. They need to feel that this could become a lawsuit.

I make another decision too.

I will use a simple brown envelope. Not a business envelope. Something inexpensive. Something that looks official. Something that looks almost like government paperwork.

As I write, I count characters. Then I count lines. Over and over. I check everything against the official rules on the Japan Post website.

While I'm writing, I also decide how much compensation I should ask for.

I include the legal references I wrote down from the seminar recording:

Copyright Act, Article 21 (Right of Reproduction)
Copyright Act, Article 23 (Right of Public Transmission)
Trademark Act, Article 25 (Exclusive Right)
Trademark Act, Article 37 (Acts Constituting Infringement)
Civil Code, Article 709 (Tort)

I write that the seller has violated these articles.

I argue copyright infringement. I argue trademark infringement. I argue damage to my brand reputation.

In some of the copied images, there is my own photograph. When that is the case, I add another claim: violation of portrait rights and publicity rights.

Looking back at what I've written, I realize I have researched much more than I thought. The letter is detailed. It includes multiple legal claims. It is much more substantial than I expected it to be.

Eventually, I finish.

I sit with the letter.

I read it again.

I look at it for a while.

Then one thought comes into my mind.

Am I really going to send this?

To be honest.

I am scared.

Taking the First Step

The next day, I go to the post office.

I have prepared everything. The certified letter is printed on four A4 pages. I made three complete sets. I have the recipient's address and my own sender information. Everything is in a clear plastic folder. I also bring my seal and the brown envelope I chose.

I thought I would need to bring cash, but I discover I can pay by credit card.

Only the larger post offices handle certified letters, so I go to one I normally never visit. It's about a ten-minute walk from my home.

When I arrive, I explain that I want to send a certified letter. I hand everything to the clerk.

Then I wait.

More than forty-five minutes pass.

Eventually the clerk comes back.

There is a problem.

The number of characters has exceeded the official limit.

The clerk hands me the instructions. I will need to correct the document.

So I go home.

I correct the document.

Then I return to the same post office later that day.

This time, everything is accepted.

Again, I wait for more than forty-five minutes.

I remember thinking how surprisingly long the process is. Later I will learn why. Several postal employees check the document together. They count every character. They count every line.

Because my certified letter is four pages long, the total cost is a little over 2,000 yen.

The postal staff themselves seem unfamiliar with certified letters.

At the counter, they staple the four pages together and stamp across the staple so the document cannot be altered.

Three copies are used.

One copy will be sent to the seller.

One copy will remain at the post office.

One copy will be returned to me as my official record.

I also receive the receipt.

I request delivery confirmation as well. I know I will need it later as evidence.

Finally.

The certified letter leaves my hands.

I walk out of the post office.

I feel relieved that I have finally completed this first step.

I didn't tell anyone.

It stayed my secret.
